## The Angularity Myth: The Rise and Fall of the "Mars Effect"

While the Carlson study dismantled the idea that a chart could predict personality, there was another, perhaps even more fascinating failure that struck specifically at the heart of "angularity"—the very mechanic astrocartography relies on. This was the saga of Michel Gauquelin and the "Mars Effect."

Gauquelin wasn’t just a skeptic looking to score points; he was a researcher who actually thought he had found the needle in the haystack. In the mid-20th century, he gathered data on thousands of eminent professionals and noticed a strange anomaly: elite athletes seemed to be born just after Mars rose or culminated (passed overhead) significantly more often than chance would predict.

For decades, this was the "smoking gun." It suggested that planets *did* have power when they were on the angles—exactly what astrocartography claims. It wasn't about zodiac signs; it was about location and timing. It looked like hard, statistical proof that the physical position of a planet relative to the horizon at birth mattered.

### The Collapse: Selection Bias and the Sociology of Time

But then, the closer science looked, the more the "Mars Effect" dissolved. When a massive, definitive replication was run by the French committee CFEPP in the 90s, using strict pre-agreed criteria to prevent cherry-picking data, the effect vanished. The athletes’ Mars placements were purely random.

However, the killing blow didn’t come from astronomy; it came from sociology. Researcher Geoffrey Dean discovered something that turned the "celestial anomaly" into a human one. He noticed that the "Mars Effect" disappeared completely in populations born after 1950.

Why? Because prior to 1950, birth times were often reported by parents to registry offices days after the event. Dean demonstrated that parents in the late 19th and early 20th centuries frequently "rounded" birth times or adjusted them to match desirable folk beliefs. The "effect" wasn’t the planet Mars pulling on the child; it was the parents fudging the paperwork. Once hospital recording became automated and precise, the "planetary influence" vanished.

### The Implication: Why We Don't Trust the "Mars Line"

This failure is devastating for standard astrocartography. If Mars rising doesn’t actually make you an athlete, then the claim that moving to a "Mars Line" (where Mars is rising) will make you energetic, competitive, or successful is baseless. It is built on a statistical mirage.

This is why The Woven Map rejects **Method 2**—the idea that natal lines on the earth have static power. We accept the debunking of the Mars Effect as proof that the "Universal Grid" does not exist.
